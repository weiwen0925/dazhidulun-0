IIIntroduction

導論

The Mahāprajñāpāramitāśāstra is the only surviving complete line-by-line commentary on the Great Perfection of Wisdom Sūtra in the Chinese Buddhist canon. In one hundred fascicles and over a million characters, it is a foundational text of the Mādhyamaka school and a cornerstone guide to Chinese Mahāyāna thought.

The treatise is traditionally attributed to Nāgārjuna. Between 402 and 405 CE (Hongshi years 4–7 of the Later Qin), Kumārajīva translated it into Chinese at the Xiaoyao Garden in Chang'an; King Yao Xing of the Later Qin took part in the collation, and it was set down by a team of over five hundred including Sengrui and Sengzhao. According to Sengrui's prefaces, the first 34 fascicles are a full translation of the opening "Preface" chapter, while from fascicle 35 ("Chapter on Recompense") onward Kumārajīva abridged the text—"because the people of Qin prefer concision"—to about one third. So although it runs to a hundred fascicles, it is in fact an epitome of the Sanskrit original.

Its guiding method is the Mādhyamaka apophatic of "neither arising nor ceasing, neither one nor many," applied phrase by phrase to the Great Prajñāpāramitā. It expounds at length the Six Perfections, the Eighteen Emptinesses, the Ten Grounds, the Thirty-seven Factors of Awakening, the Ten Powers, the Four Fearlessnesses, and the Eighteen Distinctive Qualities of a Buddha, citing copiously from the Āgamas, Jātakas, and parables. It is among the most important documents for the study of early Indian Mahāyāna thought, Chinese Prajñāpāramitā scholarship, Mādhyamaka doxography, and Kumārajīva's translation workshop.

IVPairing Studies — Essential Fascicles for Three Paths

合讀對照

As a core Mahāyāna treatise, this work can be entered by three paths: the "path of refuting views" centered on the Mūlamadhyamakakārikā, the "path of refuting own-nature" centered on the Eighteen Emptinesses, and the "path of cultivation and realization" centered on buddha-recollection. Follow any one path deeply and the whole treatise opens up.

i

Centered on the Mūlamadhyamakakārikā — Refuting Views

Madhyamaka Track

The Kārikā is the outline; this treatise is the detail. The fascicles below correspond directly to the 27 chapters and are best read alongside the Kārikā.

Centered on the Four Dhyānas & Eight Concentrations — the "stilling" gate of Prajñā

Eight Concentrations · The Stilling Track

The Eighteen Emptinesses are the insight gate of "wisdom," seeing through own-nature; the Four Dhyānas & Eight Concentrations are the calming gate of "concentration," settling the mind. Insight and calm together are the "twin track of concentration and wisdom" in the Perfection of Wisdom—so right after the Eighteen Emptinesses (wisdom) comes this complementary track of calming practice (from fascicle 17, "Explaining the Perfection of Meditation").

Dhyānas & ConcentrationsThe subtractive ladder—each step up drops something (all from fasc. 17)

Meditative concentration is not about adding anything; it is a steady subtraction—each step up releases what the previous step still clung to: from leaving the five desires all the way down to the cessation of feeling and perception.

The Four-Gate Mapping as Method — the "discernment" gate of Prajñā

Four Gates · The Dialectic Track

The Eighteen Emptinesses (wisdom) supply the checklist of "what to refute," and the Four Dhyānas & Concentrations (concentration) steady the observing mind; the Four-Gate Mapping is "how to refute"—fascicle 18 maps four dialectical sets one-to-one onto four chapters of the Kārikā, Nāgārjuna's methodological engine for refuting own-nature. Added after insight and calm, this "discernment" path completes the wisdom gate (from fascicle 18, "Explaining the Character of Prajñā").

Four GatesFour basic questions—three refutations, one recovery (all from fasc. 18)

Of anything at all you can ask only four kinds of question: itself, its relations, its origin, and how we speak of it. The first three gates each show that "presupposing own-nature leads to contradiction"; the fourth sheathes the blade with the two truths—what is refuted is own-nature, not the world. The Thirty-Seven Factors (Mahāyāna) — the "practice" gate of Prajñā

Thirty-Seven Factors · The Practice Track

The Eighteen Emptinesses (wisdom) set out "what to refute," the Four Dhyānas & Concentrations (concentration) steady "who does the refuting," and the Four-Gate Mapping (discernment) gives "how to refute"—and once discerned, the teaching must return to practice. Fascicle 19, following fascicle 18, rereads the Śrāvaka thirty-seven factors category by category: not one item is dropped, but the foundation is replaced by "because they are unattainable"—the foundations of mindfulness, right exertions, bases of power, faculties, powers, awakening-factors, and the path all stand anew on emptiness. Discernment first, practice after—both the treatise's own sequence (fasc. 18 → 19) and the sequence of understanding matched to practice; wisdom → concentration → discernment → practice thus closes into one complete arc of training.

37 Factors (Mahāyāna)The bodhisattva's universal training—"because unattainable" (all from fasc. 19)

Centered on Buddha-Recollection — Cultivation & Realization

Buddha-Recollection Track

A forerunner of the Pure Land school's treatise literature. Though not a Pure Land treatise as such, the Mahāprajñāpāramitāśāstra systematically treats the samādhi of buddha-recollection, the methods of seeing the Buddha, and the merits of the Buddha's name. The three Chinese Pure Land patriarchs (Tanluan, Daochuo, Shandao) cite it often; the three great methods of the three Pure Land sūtras—faith in the original vow, visualization recollection, and reciting the name—all find a clear theoretical forerunner here.

RationaleWhy a non-linear reading works

Reason I
The "full/abridged" structure is Kumārajīva's editorial choice
Kumārajīva states plainly that the abridgment begins at fascicle 35 (his note at the end of fasc. 34: "an abridged gathering of the treatise, digesting its essentials"). Fascicles 1–34 extend Indian Vibhāṣā-style argument and differ in style from fascicle 35 on. The treatise is itself a "full + abridged" hybrid, and one should recognize this structure while reading.
Reason II
A two-tier architecture of "outline" and "detailed commentary"
The whole structure runs: outline (fasc. 1 dependent arising, fasc. 100 entrustment) → core (fasc. 18, 31, 32: Prajñā, emptiness, dependent arising) → practice (fasc. 11–18 Six Perfections, 19–22 path-factors, 24–28 buddha-qualities) → stages (fasc. 46–47, 49, 73) → narrative finale (fasc. 96–100). You can enter at any tier.
Reason III
Yin Shun's "Madhyamaka as the guiding principle"
Yin Shun frames the treatise as "the unfolding of Nāgārjuna's Madhyamaka." The Madhyamaka core lies in fasc. 1, 18, 31, 32—master these four, and every other fascicle reveals itself as their expansion. This is the most widely accepted method in Chinese scholarship.
Reason IV
"Layered reading" in the tradition of encyclopedic treatises
The treatise inherits the Indian "encyclopedic treatise" style—Nāgārjuna cites vast amounts of sūtra, vinaya, and śāstra, plus parables, Jātakas, narratives, non-Buddhist views, and Vibhāṣā argument—an encyclopedic structure, which Kumārajīva then edited in "full/abridged" form. So it is a treatise for "layered reading," not a novel for "linear reading."

ConclusionThis is a treatise for "layered reading." Mastering the five tiers—outline → core → practice → stages → narrative—opens it up better than reading 1→100 in order. Yin Shun's layered method plus Lamotte's fascicle-group method are the two most often recommended by modern scholars and teachers.

Take away every part—is there still a "car"?—Nāgārjuna dismantles "whole vs. part"
Fascicle 20 poses a puzzle with two odd terms—the "whole" (the part-possessor) and the "part": is the whole that unifies the components a real thing, or just a name? Nāgārjuna's pincer: the whole can relate to its parts in only two ways—same or different. If same: one part would be the whole car, so one car splits into dozens; or the "whole" vanishes into a mere heap of parts. If different: strip away every part and try to point to the "car"—you can't; there is no "car without any parts." Both roads collapse, so the "whole" is not an entity over and above the parts, only a name (a convenient designation) pasted on the assembly—useful, but empty of own-nature. Apply it to the body: limbs, head, and torso are the parts, the "body" the whole; strip the parts and no "body" hides behind them. To refute the body is to refute male/female marks—not that gendered appearance vanishes, but that there is no fixed, substantial "gendered body." Told through cars, Lego, and sports teams, it loosens the grip of "my body is one solid thing"—the Madhyamaka upgrade of mindfulness of the body.

Why does deeper meditation harden the self-view?—breaking the Möbius loop of proliferation at the level of view
Fascicle 18: the non-Buddhist, "not relinquishing all dharmas, has no true wisdom." [Concentration] × [self-grasping] = infinite proliferation—concentration is only a neutral amplifier; self-grasping is the multiplier: enter samādhi → meditative bliss (craving) → hardened view (view) → self-conceit (conceit) → a rebound on emerging, a Möbius loop that tightens the more you concentrate. Adding more concentration cannot cure it (that's pouring gasoline); true wisdom is relinquishment (nothing to attain). Break the self-view at the level of view: refute "there is an 'I' attaining concentration" (no-self of persons), refute "the meditative state is real and graspable" (no-self of dharmas), subject and object both empty—pull out the nail of "self" and craving, view, and conceit lose their support, the infinite loop collapsing on the spot.

ESSAY · 19
A mathematical model of practicing amid karma—how the bodhisattva "dwells with the dark" and still sees
Fascicle 18's "lamp in a dark room" simile: dispelling dark needs no total brightness. The first lamp (the bodhisattva) dwells with the dark yet already discerns everything within its circle of light; the second lamp (the Buddha) exhausts the afflictions and sees doubly clear. The rule: seeing reality is "a local function established," not a "global switch"—so long as the lit region is non-empty, insight is truly valid within it, not revoked by surrounding ignorance. A model: on domain Ω the insight function φ holds true on the lit region L whenever L is non-empty (not requiring L = the whole); practice is the monotonic expansion of L toward the whole (the Buddha) as a dynamic limit. This is exactly the mechanism by which "practicing amid karma" and "afflictions are awakening" hold.

ESSAY · 18
Three precepts for cultivating wisdom within concentration—when joy and bliss arise, how to "be aware without stepping in"
Concentration is not merely "stillness" (that reaches only the worldly four dhyānas). The instant joy arises and the mind "licks" at it (wanting to keep it, "how remarkable I am"), it slips from abiding into attachment. Three precepts hold awareness without intervening; then the Kārikā's three contemplations serve as antidote—no-self of persons breaks conceit, no-self of dharmas breaks craving and view, arising-and-ceasing sees through the subtle and the coarse—dissolving the illusion of a substantial self and real dharmas. A workable manual for cultivating wisdom within concentration.

ESSAY · 17
"Even the water must be emptied"—the last mile of the water-simile: the emptiness of emptiness
Fascicle 17 uses "waves and still water are both H₂O" to figure the emptiness of appearances: turbulent and calm share one nature, both without self. But if you take "emptiness" as a real "water/substance" behind all things, dharma-clinging slips back in the rear door (reifying emptiness, nihilistic emptiness). Here the "emptiness of emptiness" among the eighteen empties even emptiness—emptiness is medicine set against "being"; when the illness is gone, the medicine too is dropped. Grasping not even emptiness is true non-grasping (absolute emptiness).

ESSAY · 16
"It's not that they lack it—they just can't use it"—the bodhisattva's compassion is not what you think
Same compassion, but the difference is not intensity of feeling—it is cognition. Ordinary people "pity their lack": assuming beings have nothing, they give outward, still within dualistic giving; the bodhisattva "pities that they possess a marvel yet cannot see it": knowing beings already possess meditative joy and reality, only like a rich blind man sitting on buried treasure, unknowing and unseeing. The deepest compassion is not to stuff bread into your hands but to let you see again the treasure beneath your feet—a single thought that turns private meditative joy into a boundless vow of compassion.

ESSAY · 15
"No one is born a sage"—so is the reincarnate lama a false premise?
Fascicle 17: uncontaminated concentration does not appear ready-made at rebirth; it must be re-evoked this life by uncontaminated wisdom. But this denies automatic "manifest activity," not the "continuity" of roots of goodness, habituation, and vow-power. Reincarnation, rightly understood, is exactly continuity + re-evocation this life—far from being denied, it is stated more clearly; and deeper still, the Madhyamaka says even "sage" is a dependently-arisen designation.

ESSAY · 14
The samādhi of non-dispute is not "drilled into being"—what does "cultivating without cultivating" really mean?
People assume samādhi-power is all accumulated by drill. But the Madhyamaka says non-dispute is the natural manifesting of "absolute emptiness": dispute arises from three walls built by self-view (self-grasping, dharma-grasping, object-grasping); one flash of absolute emptiness leaves the walls no ground. Cultivating without cultivating—what you cultivate is "seeing emptiness" (tearing down walls); non-dispute is what remains once the walls are gone, not something separately built.

ESSAY · 13
After "truly nothing attained," how can one still cultivate and still attain?—supplying the bridge in Madhyamaka's refute-and-establish
Really existent → no need to attain; originally non-existent → cannot attain—the dilemma exhausts the self-nature of "attainment," yielding the ultimate "truly nothing attained." But this is not annihilation: emptiness negates "self-nature," not the "workings of dependent arising." Precisely because there is no self-nature can conditions gather and "attainment as designation" vividly appear. Empty, conventional, middle—the three are one.

Meditation is not reaching up for higher states but dismantling "I am watching X" layer by layer—the key to fascicle 17

From the four dhyānas to infinite consciousness, Nāgārjuna writes the whole meditative ladder as a layer-by-layer dismantling of "subject-object opposition": ascent is not gaining a higher state but subtraction—prying apart, level by level, the crack of "I am watching it."

"The base of infinite space" is not a place—you may have been misreading this passage all along
"Entering the base of infinite space" is often misread as "going to a place called space." In fact "ceasing the mark of resistance" is unloading, from consciousness, the concept that "objects occupy space"—entering an unobstructed mode of cognition. Not a location, but the removal of a cognition.

The unit of suffering is "motion"—joining the dhyāna subtraction to "motion is suffering," with a fail-safe against reifying the permanent
Pleasure is suffering, and the reason is "motion"; suffering = the mind's momentum, so reducing motion reduces suffering. But going from motion to stillness, if you grasp "stillness" as a real, permanent thing, you fall into eternalism again—the true middle way is: grasp neither motion nor stillness.
